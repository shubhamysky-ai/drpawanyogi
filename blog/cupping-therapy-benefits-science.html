What is Cupping Therapy?

Most physical therapies (like massage, dry needling, or manual mobilization) apply positive pressure—compressing muscle tissue downwards. Cupping therapy is unique because it applies **negative pressure**.

By placing cups on the skin and creating a vacuum (using heat or a suction pump), the skin, fascia, and superficial muscle layers are drawn upward into the cup. This decompresses the tissues underneath, lifting them away from the bone and stretching the tight fascial sheath.

The Scientific Benefits of Cupping

Though it looks simple, the suction effect triggers profound physiological changes:

  • Myofascial Decompression: Fascia is a web-like sheath wrapping around all muscles. Poor posture or injury makes fascia sticky, binding muscles and restricting movement. Suction pulls fascia away from the muscle, breaking structural adhesions.
  • Dramatically Increased Blood Flow: The vacuum dilates blood vessels, pulling fresh oxygen-rich blood into the tight muscle fibers, speeding up cellular recovery.
  • Lymphatic Drainage: Helps flush metabolic waste, lactic acid, and inflammatory fluids out of muscle tissues into the lymphatic system.
  • Pain Gateway Interruption: Stimulates sensory nerve endings in the skin, which overrides the nervous system's chronic pain signals.

What Do the Circular Marks Mean?

Contrary to popular belief, these marks are not bruises caused by blunt impact. The suction draws stagnant blood and metabolic waste up from deep tissues to the surface, where the lymphatic system can process it. The darker the mark, the higher the stagnation in that muscle. They are completely painless and fade away within 3 to 7 days.

Dry vs. Wet Cupping: What is the Difference?

At our clinic, we perform different types of cupping based on patient requirements:

  • Dry Cupping: Suction is created on target areas and left static for 5 to 10 minutes to release deep trigger points.
  • Dynamic (Gliding) Cupping: We apply therapeutic oils to the skin and slide the suction cups along muscle fibers. This works as an intensive deep-tissue massage, releasing large bands of fascia.
  • Wet Cupping (Hijama): Involves making tiny superficial scratches on the skin before applying a second suction, drawing out tiny amounts of stagnant blood. *Performed strictly under sterile clinical protocols.*
