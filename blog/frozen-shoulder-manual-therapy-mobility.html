What is a Frozen Shoulder?

Frozen shoulder is a condition characterized by stiffness, pain, and restricted motion in the shoulder joint. It occurs when the strong connective tissue surrounding the shoulder joint (known as the joint capsule) becomes inflamed, thickens, and tightens. This restricts the space inside the joint and makes movement painful.

It is highly common in individuals aged 40 to 60, especially in diabetic patients, or those recovering from a shoulder fracture, stroke, or surgery that limited shoulder movements for a long time.

The Three Phases of Frozen Shoulder

Unlike sudden injuries, Frozen Shoulder develops gradually in three distinct stages:

  1. The Freezing (Painful) Stage: Lasts 2 to 9 months. Any movement of your shoulder causes sharp pain, and the shoulder slowly begins to lose its range of motion. Pain is often worse at night.
  2. The Frozen (Stiff) Stage: Lasts 4 to 12 months. The pain may start to diminish, but the shoulder becomes extremely stiff. Using the arm becomes highly challenging.
  3. The Thawing (Recovery) Stage: Lasts 5 months to 2 years. The ability to move the shoulder slowly begins to improve, and full or near-full mobility is restored.

Why Diabetic Patients are More Vulnerable?

High blood sugar levels can trigger a process called "glycation" in the body, where sugar molecules attach to collagen fibers in the joint capsule. This makes the tissue sticky and brittle, significantly increasing the likelihood of developing Adhesive Capsulitis.

How Physiotherapy Resolves Frozen Shoulder

Without intervention, a frozen shoulder can take up to 2 years to thaw, causing immense disruption to daily life. Specialized physical therapy can compress this recovery timeline to just a few weeks.

  • Joint Mobilization: Dr. Pawan Yogi applies specialized manual mobilization techniques (like Maitland or Mulligan concepts) to stretch the tight capsule and restore joint gliding.
  • Passive Stretching: Gentle, therapist-assisted stretching to reach movement angles that are impossible for the patient to perform on their own.
  • Modalities for Pain Management: Using ultrasound, heat therapy, or TENS (Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation) to ease pain before starting manual mobilization.
  • Active Exercises: Incorporating shoulder pulley exercises, wand stretches, and finger-wall climbs to continuously expand joint mobility.
  • Strengthening: Once mobility returns, we introduce light resistance bands to strengthen the rotator cuff and stabilize the joint.
